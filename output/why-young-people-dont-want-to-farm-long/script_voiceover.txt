WHY YOUNG PEOPLE DON'T WANT TO FARM
Voiceover script — long-form (~2:40)

HOW TO READ THIS:
- New line          = small breath.
- Blank line        = full pause (1 beat). Let the silence sit. Don't rush it.
- Tone              = calm, slow, certain. Like telling ONE person a true thing at 5am.
- Before recording  = read it out loud 3-4 times. Circle any word that trips you.

============================================================

Think about the last time you ate a bowl of rice.
You didn't wonder who grew it.
Almost no one does.


One number changed how I see it.
In America, the average farmer is fifty-eight years old.
More than forty percent are over sixty-five.


In Thailand, where rice paddies once passed from parent to child,
the story is the same.
The land is aging.
The children left.


So who grows the food next?

============================================================

The answer starts with math.


Farming costs years.
Years of weather that turns,
harvests that fail,
seasons you bet on and lose.


And when the money comes, it comes at harvest.
Never on payday.


A young person who leaves a Thai village for Bangkok
can earn far more in a year.
Not because the city is better.
Because a paycheck is something you can point to.


A failed harvest is something you carry alone.

============================================================

But the math isn't the whole story.


Even if the income were fair, there's something else.
An invisible tax.


In Thailand, and across most of Southeast Asia,
the farm is where you came from.
Not where you're going.


Generations of parents who worked the soil
sent their children to school with one quiet prayer.
Please, not this.


Degrees meant desks.
Desks meant something better.


There's no LinkedIn profile for organic farmer.
No performance review for growing food without chemicals.


The work I do,
the five AM wake-ups,
the soil under my fingernails,
the rows I walk before breakfast,
none of it shows in the numbers the world uses to measure a life.

============================================================

We built a world that rewards what it can count.
And what you grow in the ground is hard to count.

============================================================

I am one of the people who stayed.


Every morning I walk our rows,
check the soil with my hands,
smell the water before the rows come into focus.


Then I come inside and open a laptop.
I'm teaching myself to code.
I'm building with AI.


Not to leave the farm.
Because the farm is exactly where that kind of building belongs.


The farm taught me patience.
The code taught me precision.
They are the same skill.

============================================================

So why don't young people want to farm?


Maybe the real question is,
what did we teach them to want instead?


We told them the numbers were wrong.
We told them the soil was beneath them.
We never told them that what grows in the ground
can grow into something new.

============================================================

Someone grew that bowl of rice.
Someone who stayed when every signal told them to leave.


The bravest generation of farmers
isn't the one that planted the past.
It's the one brave enough to plant
something the world hasn't seen yet.

============================================================

TRICKY WORDS (say each one slow, a few times, before you record):
- harvest      -> HAR-vist
- soil         -> rhymes with "boil"
- fingernails  -> FING-ger-nails
- measure      -> MEH-zher
- beneath      -> bih-NEETH
- precision    -> prih-SIH-zhun
- generation   -> jeh-neh-RAY-shun
- chemicals    -> KEM-ih-kulz

DELIVERY TIPS:
- Slower than feels natural. Your calm IS the brand. Other channels rush; you don't.
- Let your pitch fall slightly at the end of each line. It sounds certain, not unsure.
- The blank-line pauses are where the viewer feels something. Do not fill them.
- Smile a little on the warm lines (rice, morning, soil). The mic hears the smile.
- One line at a time is fine. You can record line by line and I'll stitch them.
