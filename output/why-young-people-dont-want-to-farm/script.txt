The next time you sit down to eat, look at your plate. Someone grew that food with their hands.

But almost nobody asks who. And the answer is strange. In America, the average farmer is now fifty-eight years old — and more than forty percent are over sixty-five. Here in Thailand, it's no different. The people who feed us are quietly running out of time.

So where is the next generation? I know — because I'm part of it. I grew up on an organic farm in Thailand. And one by one, I watched everyone my age leave. They went to the city. To offices. To screens. To money that arrives the same day. Farming looked like the past. Slow. Dirty. Invisible. I almost left too.

But every morning, I still walk out before the sun. The soil is cold under my feet. I can smell the water before I can see the rows.

And here's what no one told us: this isn't the past. While I plant, I'm teaching myself to code — to build with AI. The future doesn't have to replace the soil. It can grow out of it.

So the next time you look at your plate, remember: someone chose to stay. Maybe the bravest thing my generation can do isn't leaving the land. It's coming back to it.
